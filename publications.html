Color Data

I did this project for the Computatioanl fabrication Group at MIT. The data presented here is used in their research on appearance fabrication. For confidentiality reasons, I cannot give more details on the nataure of their project. For this work, I made 280 oil paint color swatches with specific pigment concentrations. Each swatch is about 1 x 1 inch (~ 2 x 2 cm) large and about 1/10 inch (2 mm) thick. Color transparency was an important factor, therefore, I tried to choose paints that are more transparent. I mixed the following 8 tubes of premium oil paint from Old Holland :

Scheveningen yellow lemon (Pigment: Monoazo PY3)
Cadmium yellow medium (Pigment: Cadmium zinc sulphide PY35)
Scarlet lake extra (Pigments: Anthanthrone PR168 - Quinacridone PR209)
Alizarin crimson lake extra (Pigments: Quinacridone PV19 - Anthraquinone PBR23)
Cobalt Blue (Pigments: Oxides of Cobalt and Aluminium PB28)
French ultramarine light extra (Pigments: Synthetic ultramarine PB29 - oxide of cobalt and aluminium PB28)
Viridian green deep (Pigment: Chromium hydroxide PG18)
Mixed White (Mixed white number 2: zinc and titanium)

From the producers website: "Lake" means transparent glazing colours, "Extra" means traditional colour made from lightfast pigment.

Please note that I have no affiliation with this brand. I chose it because of its consistency and pigment quality, based on recommendations from artists teaching at the Museum of Fine Arts of Boston.

You can view the palette with all the 280 mixed colors here. If you click on any of the colors, it will take you to the detailed specifications of that color swatch.

Swatch Preparation

My objective was to make swatches with known concentration of each of the mixing paints. For this reason, I needed to weigh the share of each paint in the mixture. I used the following scale for the measurements: Smart Weigh Pro Pocket Scale, Readability: 0.01 gram (0.001 oz). I used disposable laboratory sample papers to weigh and mix the paints on. The example below explains the procedure:

Example: to make a medium green (Data number 100 below) with 1 part Cadmium yellow medium and 1 part Viridian green, I take a new sample paper and put it on the scale and then calibrate the scale on 0.00 gram. Next, I add the Cadmium yellow medium paint until the scale stabilizes and shows 1.00 gram. Then I add Viridian green to it until the scale stabilizes and shows 2.00 grams. I take the sample paper off the scale and mix the two paints together thoroughly using a mixing knife. I take some of this mixture with the knife and spread it on the canvas paper.

After one sheet of canvas paper is filled with swatches, I put it at the bottom of a plastic container and close the lid, to keep it safe from any possible dust or contamination, and allow it to dry for a few weeks.



Simple Color Theory

Here are some quick notes for the attention of the non-color scientist reader. My source for the information is the following book: "Vision and Art, The Biology of Seeing" by Margaret Livingstone, ISBN 978-0-8109-9554-3. It is a very interesting and informative book for artists.

Light, in form of electromagnetic waves, come down from the sun (and other illumination sources) to us. Each electromagnetic wave can be characterized using its "wavelength". (Because we like to assign a "number" to everything so we can work with them easier). Those electromagnetic waves that have a wavelength between 400 nm and 700 nm can be detected by human eyes and can be interpreted in our brains as "colors". Therefore, we refer to the range of 400 nm to 700 nm as the visible spectrum.

For example, an electromagnetic wave with wavelength equal to about 500 nm is usually interpreted in our brain as color green. The reason for this "usually" is that, it depends on the beholder! We are all different in terms of light receptors that we have in our eyes and the way our brain interprets the signals coming from these receptors. That's why a green leaf can look brown to a some color-blind people. To solve this problem, the International Commission on Illumination (CIE) came up with a standard observer. Color scientists use this standard observer in their calculations for consistency. The rendered image below shows the associated color that the standard observer sees by looking at light of different wavelengths in the visible spectrum. We can understand from the image that when we see an object as "green", it means that the object is reflecting mostly the light at wavelengths about 500 nm to 550 nm.

When some rays of light from an illuminant (sun, light bulb, etc) hits an object, the object transmits or absorbs some parts of this light, and reflects the rest of it to our eyes. Therefore, the light that reaches our eyes varies depending on the object that reflects it, but also the type of illuminant. Once again, for consistency reasons, the CIE defines standard illuminants. For example, the illuminant D65, which is used in the calculations in this work, is defined to resemble average daylight. Here is the explanation from CIE (source: http://www.cie.co.at/publ/abst/s005.html):

CIE standard illuminant D65
(Joint ISO/CIE Standard, ISO 10526:1999/CIE S005/E-1998, CIE Standard Illuminants for Colorimetry)
"This is intended to represent average daylight and has a correlated colour temperature of approximately 6 500 K. CIE standard illuminant D65 should be used in all colorimetric calculations requiring representative daylight... Variations in the relative spectral power distribution of daylight are known to occur as a function of season, time of day, and geographic location. However, CIE standard illuminant D65 should be used pending the availability of additional information on these variations."

For each of the color swatches, I have included in the Swatch Specifications section a plot that shows reflectance factor of the color at different wavelengths. A light spectrum is put as the background of the graph. An example will show you how to interpret the graph: Consider data number 100. It is made of 1 part cadmium yellow medium and 1 part viridian green. We can see in the graph that this color swatch is reflecting mostly the light with wavelength between 500 nm and 550 nm (the peak of the graph), which corresponds to the green light as seen in the background visible spectrum. We can also see that the graph goes through relatively high values of reflectance at wavelengths corresponding to the yellow color, because of the presence of cadmium yellow. Data number 101. is made of 9 parts cadmium yellow medium and 1 part viridian green. We can see that the graph has retained the general shape of the previous graph because our constituting pigments in these two swatches are similar. However, the graph moves towards warmer colors and goes through higher values of reflectance factor, because the addition of cadmium yellow has made the color warmer and has lightened up the color (Higher reflectance factor means more light reaching to our eyes, which means "lighter").

Color Analysis

I used the Xrite i1Pro 2 Spectrophotometer for the measurements. Full technical specifications can be found at the manufacturere's website

I calculated the X Y Z tristimulus values using the Reflectance factor at 400 to 700 nm, in 10 nm steps, CIE 2nd observer, and CIE D65 illuminant. I calculated the L* and used it to represent the lightness (vlaue) of each swatch. I then calculated the Standard RGB (CIE 2nd observer) for each swatch. The colors shown in this website are the result of inputting these values into the the HTML RGB function. Appointments to view the actual swtaches can be arranged, please contact me for more information.
